Why this matters

1. Open the app or webpage that controls your smart device.

üîë Password Hygiene

Do you use different passwords for important accounts (email, banking, shopping)?
‚ñº
Do you use different passwords for important accounts?
Why this matters
If one site is breached, re-used passwords can let attackers into your other accounts.
How to check

1) List your key accounts; note if any share the same password.
2) If yes, change them to unique passwords (use a manager for convenience).

Do you use a password manager to store and generate passwords?
‚ñº
Do you use a password manager to store and generate passwords?
Why this matters
Managers reduce password re-use and help you use stronger, unique passwords without needing to remember them all.
How to check

1) If you store passwords in notes or reuse them, install a manager (e.g., built-in browser store or dedicated app).
2) Move key accounts into the manager and enable autofill/alerts.

Do you use two-factor authentication (2FA) on your important accounts (email, banking, socials)??
‚ñº
Do you use two-factor authentication (2FA) on your important accounts (email, banking, socials)??
Why this matters
2FA adds an extra layer of security, making it harder for attackers to access your accounts even if they get your password.
How to check

1) Open account Security settings (email/bank/social).
2) Enable Two-Factor Authentication (use an authenticator app over SMS if available).
3) Store backup codes safely.

Have you changed the default router admin password and your Wi-Fi network name (SSID)?
Why this matters
Default router passwords are widely known and often targeted by attackers to take over your network. Changing the SSID also makes it harder to guess your router model and default settings.
How to check

1) Log into your router app or web portal (often by typing 192.168.0.1 or 192.168.1.1 into a browser).
2) Login and change admin password; then rename SSID and set a strong Wi-Fi password (WPA2/WPA3).

Is your router firmware and software apps up to date (automatic updates enabled where available)?
‚ñº
Is your router firmware and software apps up to date (automatic updates enabled where available)?
Why this matters
Updates fix (patch) known vulnerabilities to prevent attackers actively exploiting them .
How to check

Router firmware:
1) Log into your router app or web portal (often by typing 192.168.0.1 or 192.168.1.1 into a browser).
2) Look for a menu called Administration, System, or Firmware.
3) Turn on Auto-update if available, or click Check for updates and apply the update.

Apps and software:
1) On your phone/tablet, open the App Store (iOS) or Google Play Store (Android).
2) Go to Manage apps & updates (Android) or Account ‚Üí Updates (iOS).
3) Enable Automatic Updates in the store settings, or manually update apps you use to control smart devices.
4) On PCs/laptops, make sure Windows Update or macOS Software Update is turned on.

‚ñº
Is your Wi-Fi set up safely (Guest Wi-Fi for visitors, WPS turned off, and unknown devices removed)?
Why this matters
Guest Wi-Fi keeps visitors and IoT separate from your main devices. Turning off WPS closes an easy way in. Removing unknown devices stops unauthorised access.
How to check

1) Log into your router app or web portal (often by typing 192.168.0.1 or 192.168.1.1 into a browser).
2) Look for Wireless/Guest Network, enable it and give it a different password.
3) In Wireless/Advanced, find WPS and set it to Off (or disable PIN).
4) Open Connected Devices/Device List, rename things you recognise, and remove/block anything unfamiliar.

‚ñº
Would you recognise a phishing message?
Why this matters
Phishing is a common way criminals steal passwords and money. Spotting the signs (odd sender, urgent threats, suspicious links) prevents most attacks.
How to check

1) Be wary of messages that are unexpected, urgent, or ask for personal info.
2) Check the sender address carefully (not just the display name).
3) Hover over links (or long-press on mobile) to preview the web address: if it looks odd, don‚Äôt click.
4) If unsure, contact the organisation using a phone number or website you trust (not the link in the message).

Do you check links and use official apps/sites?
Why this matters
Fake login pages can look real but steal your password. Going directly to the official site/app avoids this trap.
How to check

1) For important accounts, open the official app, or type the website (e.g., www.yourbank.co.uk) into your browser.
2) Check the address bar: spelling, padlock, and the correct domain (e.g., .co.uk for UK sites).
3) If a message says ‚Äúverify‚Äù or ‚Äúyour parcel is waiting‚Äù, don‚Äôt click the link ‚Äî go to the official site/app yourself.
